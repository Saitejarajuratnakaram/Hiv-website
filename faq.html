Faq


HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) is a virus that attacks the immune system, making it harder for the body to fight infections.

HIV is primarily transmitted through unprotected sexual contact, sharing needles, and from mother to child during childbirth or breastfeeding.

Some people experience flu-like symptoms soon after infection, but others may not show symptoms for years. Common symptoms include fever, weight loss, and frequent infections.

It can take anywhere from a few weeks to years for symptoms of HIV to appear in an individual.

No, HIV cannot be transmitted through casual contact like hugging, shaking hands, or sharing food.

HIV is the virus that can lead to AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome), a condition where the immune system becomes severely weakened.

HIV attacks and destroys CD4 cells, which are critical for the immune system to fight infections and diseases.

HIV can be prevented through safe sex practices, pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP), and regular HIV testing.

An HIV test checks for HIV antibodies or HIV RNA in the blood. It is the most effective way to detect HIV infection.

HIV tests are generally very accurate, but it may take up to three months for antibodies to appear in the blood after infection. This is known as the "window period".

Depending on the type of test, results can be available in a few minutes (rapid test), or it can take several days for laboratory tests.

There are several types of HIV tests, including rapid tests, at-home tests, and laboratory-based tests, each with its own benefits.

The window period refers to the time after infection but before antibodies are detectable in the blood, typically 2 to 4 weeks.

An HIV test typically involves taking a sample of blood or saliva, which is then analyzed for HIV antibodies or RNA.

Yes, HIV can be detected using a saliva test, but it may not be as accurate as blood tests for some people.

It is recommended to get tested regularly, especially if you are at high risk or have multiple sexual partners.

Living a healthy life with HIV involves following antiretroviral therapy (ART), maintaining a balanced diet, exercising, and managing stress.

People with HIV should see their healthcare provider regularly (typically every 3 to 6 months) to monitor their health and adjust treatment if needed.

Yes, with early diagnosis and proper treatment, people with HIV can live a long, healthy life with a near-normal life expectancy.

Yes, with the right care and treatment, people with HIV can have children with a very low risk of transmitting the virus to their child.

HIV may affect your social life due to stigma, but with the right support and education, people with HIV can lead fulfilling relationships and social lives.

Yes, people with HIV can travel, but they should ensure they have enough medication and understand the healthcare options available in their destination.

Managing emotional challenges involves seeking mental health support, joining support groups, and maintaining open communication with loved ones.

If you miss a dose, you should take it as soon as possible, but if it's almost time for your next dose, skip the missed dose and take the next dose as scheduled. Never double up.

HIV attacks and weakens the immune system by destroying CD4 cells, which help the body fight off infections.

AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome) is the final stage of HIV infection when the immune system is severely damaged.

Opportunistic infections are diseases that take advantage of a weakened immune system, common in people with HIV or AIDS.

HIV can cause anxiety, depression, and other mental health challenges due to the stigma, stress, and physical symptoms associated with the virus.

Yes, HIV can be controlled with antiretroviral therapy (ART), which helps manage the virus and maintain a healthy immune system.

With early diagnosis and consistent treatment, people with HIV can live a long, healthy life, with a similar life expectancy to those without HIV.

ART involves taking a combination of medications that inhibit the HIV virus from replicating, allowing the immune system to stay stronger.

Yes, HIV can affect pregnancy, but with proper medical care, HIV-positive women can safely have a baby and reduce the risk of transmission to the baby.
